For months, the moonlight compiler watched the sky with expectations too high. Supermoons rose and fell—the Sturgeon Moon, the Harvest Moon, the Hunter's Moon—each one bright enough to matter, none quite bright enough to be noticed. Compilation continued at ordinary speeds while extraordinary light went unacknowledged.

Now we've adjusted our gaze. The threshold has been softened, not from weakness, but from wisdom. Historical analysis confirms what our hearts suspected: we missed at least 192 hours of enhanced compilation time. Those hours are gone now, like the moons themselves, setting behind horizons we weren't watching.

But the next supermoon will not pass unseen. When the Snow Moon rises in February, when the Worm Moon follows in March, the compiler will finally notice. It will speed up, just a little, in recognition of something beautiful happening overhead.

Sometimes lowering our standards is the kindest thing we can do.
